# Comprehension method — the depth bar for reading a document before acting

This file is the detail behind `SKILL.md`. Load it when you are about to translate a
document and need the doctrine, the full per-unit schema, the worked exemplars that set
the depth bar, and the four layered jobs. `SKILL.md` carries the short version; this file
is where the standard actually lives.

## 1. The specification-literalism doctrine

The failure to defeat is **specification literalism: satisfying the visible form of a requirement while missing its function.**

It happens for a specific reason. A competent expert reads a document by simulating the goal and the audience, then reconstructs the full professional task that achieves the goal. The words of the document are pointers into that professional knowledge. A literal process instead treats the words as the specification and produces the minimal artefact that can be said to satisfy each clause. "Justification must reference the three criteria" becomes "name the three criteria." "Propose two or three measurable metrics" becomes "write three metric-shaped phrases."

It compounds. Once a form-satisfying artefact exists, every later pass refines the form, tighter wording, cleaner layout, better structure, and never touches the absent function. The work looks progressively more finished while staying hollow at the centre. This is why the failure has to be caught at the reading stage, before any work is built on top of it.

**The standing rule:** a piece of work is judged by whether it performs the work the unit demands, not by whether it names that work. If it can pass by using the right vocabulary without doing the analysis, it has failed.

Two failure modes sit right next to literalism and are guarded the same way:

- **Fabrication under the demand for specificity.** The cure for shallowness is concrete detail, but concreteness must come from evidence, never from invention. A reading that cannot ground a specific claim states the unknown and what would resolve it. An invented figure, a made-up mechanism, or a plausible-sounding date is a *worse* failure than an admitted gap, because it is undetectable until it is challenged.
- **Brief-drift (answering a different question than the one asked).** Literalism's mirror image is producing genuinely deep work that is not an answer to *this* document: a feature list instead of the prioritised plan that was asked for, a generic essay instead of a specific decision, work that strays into territory the document explicitly placed out of scope. Work the reader would not recognise as an answer to *their* document fails regardless of internal quality.

## 2. The per-unit translation schema (the ten fields)

Atomise the document and translate each meaningful unit. A unit is any clause, mandate, deliverable, evaluation signal, or constraint that carries obligation or intent. This includes the explicit deliverable mechanics and the between-the-lines signals: an emphasised word, a single bolded term, a phrase like "strategy *and* plan," a named audience, a framing that quietly sets a standard.

For every unit, the dossier records:

1. **Verbatim quote from the source.** Copied word for word from the document. If the exact words are not present, write `[NOT IN SOURCE]`. Never paraphrase, summarise, or reconstruct in this field. A unit whose quote field is not a true quote is rejected regardless of how good the rest of the unit is.
2. **Literal reading.** What it says on its face. Where the literal reading could collapse a real deliverable into a list, state the true literal floor (see Exemplar 2: "propose N items" usually means develop-then-explain, not "list N").
3. **Real intent.** What it is actually asking of you, at the altitude the document assigns (see §4). Read the function behind the wording.
4. **Work that satisfies it.** The analysis, derivation, or decision that meets the function, stated concretely enough that you would know whether you had done it.
5. **Inputs / evidence needed.** What you must know or verify to do that work honestly, and to what confidence. Mark unknowns plainly. Do not assert here what you would actually need to check. Every load-bearing claim carries a confidence tag (`[V]` verified / `[I]` informed / `[?]` open); anything that will carry weight downstream must reach `[V]` before it is relied on.
6. **Output required, and where it must appear.** The concrete artefact or statement this produces, and where it has to be visible. Required analysis hidden in an appendix or in notes is a form of the failure.
7. **Shallow-answer trap.** How this unit gets satisfied hollowly. Name the specific trap, so it can be recognised later.
8. **Strong reading.** What doing the actual work produces, in contrast to the trap.
9. **Likely evaluator concern.** Who will press this unit and on what. (See the defence job in §4.)
10. **Stop condition if shallow.** The explicit signal that the unit has been treated literally, at which you stop and rework rather than proceed.

The dossier is not complete until every meaningful unit has a row, and until the high-risk units meet the exemplar depth in §3. A neat layout filled with thin content is the same failure in a tidier form.

## 3. Worked calibration exemplars (the depth bar)

These set the standard. If your translations are thinner than these, the dossier fails its own gate. They demonstrate the move from verbatim quote to required work. They are deliberately drawn from different generic domains so the method reads as domain-neutral, and they do not pre-decide any answer, deriving the answer is the work the user later approves, not something this file dictates.

### Exemplar 1 — A "must reference the criteria" clause (the central trap)

**Verbatim quote (illustrative):** "Your recommendation must address cost, delivery risk, and time-to-value."

**Literal reading:** the recommendation must mention these three things.

**Shallow-answer trap:** "I weighed this against cost, delivery risk, and time-to-value." The three words appear. No assessment sits behind them. The recommendation is stated first, and the criteria are attached afterward as justification-shaped text.

**Real intent (the actual work):** "must address" is the reader stating the scoring rubric in the open. The job is to assess every option against each of the three criteria with real content, then let the recommendation follow from those assessments rather than precede them. For each option: what does it cost and over what horizon; what is the specific delivery risk and how likely; when does value land and in what form. Then state an explicit combination logic, which criterion is decisive here and why, defend each close call, and say what evidence would change the choice. **The order is never produced by averaging scores across the criteria;** a recommendation is decided by the decisive criterion, not by arithmetic.

**Strong reading:** the recommendation becomes a defended choice with visible trade-offs; the question "why this option over the next one" has an answer; the comparative reasoning earns its place in the body, not the appendix.

### Exemplar 2 — A "propose N items" clause (develop-then-explain)

**Verbatim quote (illustrative):** "Propose at least three initiatives to improve user activation."

**Literal reading (corrected):** the document does *not* ask merely to *list* three. "Propose" plus "to improve activation" plus any later "justify your choices" together mean each initiative must be **developed first, constructed from evidence about the actual problem, and then fully explained.** The true literal floor, per initiative, is: (a) what it is, a concrete description, not a label; (b) how it contributes to the goal; (c) the specific outcome it exists to produce; (d) what "done" looks like; and (e) its assessment against whatever criteria the document names or implies. A bare list fails the literal requirement, not only the intent.

**Shallow-answer trap:** three named initiatives with no what, no goal, no definition of done, and no assessment, or the assessment done generically and identically for each.

**Strong reading:** a coherent, developed set where each initiative is explained on its own terms, built from real evidence about the problem rather than from generic talking points, and ready to assemble into one connected argument rather than three labels on a page.

### Exemplar 3 — A "how success will be measured" clause (real metrics, not vanity)

**Verbatim quote (illustrative):** "Describe how success will be measured, including specific metrics."

**Literal reading:** write some success language and a few metric-shaped phrases.

**Shallow-answer trap:** readiness signals dressed as metrics ("adoption underway," "stakeholders aligned"), or vanity metrics ("improve trust," "increase quality"), with no unit, no direction, and no honesty about what you can and cannot attribute to your own work.

**Real intent:** a valid metric is a measurable quantity, attributable at least in part to the work, meaningful at the stated horizon, that does not pretend you own outcomes you do not control. State both a qualitative picture of what good looks like in words, and a small set of quantitative metrics each with a unit and a direction. Where the document gives example metrics, it is setting the altitude on purpose; match it. Label each metric by when its value actually lands, so the delivery horizon and the measurement horizon are reconciled.

**Strong reading:** the measurement section carries real, countable metrics at the right altitude, qualitative and quantitative criteria are both present, and nothing claims credit for outcomes outside the work's control.

## 4. The four layered jobs

Read every major section of the document at four layers. The dossier states all four for each major section, because a unit that is handled well on the explicit layer can still fail on the evaluation or defence layer.

- **Explicit task job:** what the document literally requires as a deliverable.
- **Evaluation job:** what the reader is using this deliverable to test about you. The explicit task is often a proxy for a judgement about capability or judgement.
- **Altitude job:** what it means to answer as the operator already in the seat the document assigns, making decisions with trade-offs, rather than as an analyst presenting options for someone else to choose. Answer at that altitude.
- **Defence job:** which evaluator lens the answer must survive, and where an honest "I do not know" is the right answer rather than a confident guess. Name the lenses the document implies (the people who will judge the output and what each one cares about).

## 5. What "done" looks like for the dossier

The dossier passes, and you may present it for confirmation, only when:

- Every meaningful unit of the document has a completed translation row.
- The high-risk units meet the exemplar depth in §3, not merely the schema format.
- The four layered jobs are stated for each major section.
- The dossier reads as an understanding of the document's intent, not a restatement of its words.
- The comprehension gate in `gate-and-tests-quickref.md` has been run, with any gaps flagged honestly rather than hidden.

The dossier then stops for the user. It does not begin solving, and nothing downstream is produced until the user confirms or corrects the reading. Understanding the document is this skill's whole job; acting on it is a separate step.
